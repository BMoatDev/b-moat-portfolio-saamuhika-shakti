01 — What we heard

You're not building a report. You're making an argument.

That argument has been proven. It has data, it has partners, it has five years of on-ground work behind it. What it needs now is a form that makes a funder in Stockholm, a policymaker in Delhi, and a designer at a fashion brand feel the weight of it — and understand the opportunity.

That's not a production problem. That's a storytelling problem. And storytelling is what B-Moat was built for.

The argument

A garment discarded in a Bengaluru household doesn't simply disappear — it enters a system held together by the hands and expertise of informal waste pickers. These workers are not peripheral to the circular economy. They are central to it. And when we design for their dignity, we design better systems for everyone.

"This report will showcase that people- and planet-centred initiatives can go hand in hand, and that circularity can be inclusive — it needs to be."

We read that line in your brief and recognised it immediately. It's not a summary of what the report will say. It's the thesis that needs to be made undeniable. Everything — the layout, the illustration, the copy, the journey map, the web page, the animation — should be in service of that single argument becoming self-evident by the last page.

That's how we intend to approach this, if you choose to work with us.

02 — Who we are

A story-first studio. Small, deliberate, Bengaluru-based.

B-Moat is a small, distributed creative studio. We work across graphic design, illustration, copywriting, web design and development, motion, and brand strategy. We're not a large agency with a pitch team and a delivery team — the people who talk to you are the people who make the work.

Our positioning — story first — means we don't pick up a design tool until we understand what the work needs to say, to whom, and why it matters that it's said now. We've found that clients who come to us with complex, layered problems get better outcomes when we spend time at the beginning asking uncomfortable questions about narrative rather than rushing to visual solutions.

We want to be transparent about one thing: we haven't designed an impact report before. What we have done is build the component capabilities that a report like this requires — visual storytelling, long-form copywriting, systems illustration, web delivery, and motion — for clients in the sustainability and social impact space. We're not learning the territory from scratch. We're applying proven skills to a new form, in close collaboration with you.

That's a different kind of risk from hiring an agency that has done twenty reports but treats this as the twenty-first. We will bring genuine curiosity and creative investment to every page. You'll be the first people to see work like this from us — which means you'll also get our best.

03 — The work

What we've built, and what it proves.

Below is the work most relevant to this project — selected not for variety but for what each piece demonstrates about our specific capabilities for the Saamuhika Shakti textile waste report.

01
Film
Microbeworks Scientific — H&M Global Change Award
End-to-end film production
Microbeworks Scientific is a Bengaluru biotech venture developing microbial dyes as a sustainable replacement for synthetic textile dyes — eliminating one of the most toxic processes in the fashion supply chain.

B-Moat was brought in to produce the grant application film for the H&M Global Change Award. We handled everything: ideation, scripting, field shoots, studio recording, and final edit — working directly with the two founders throughout, mainly with Suchitha Raghunathan.

The film opens with colour as cultural language — Holi, classical dance, a child's first brushstrokes. It then names the harm: synthetic dyes poisoning water bodies, stripping dignity from textile workers, destroying ecosystems. It closes with the founders' vision of a textile industry that restores its relationship with nature.

The venture was supported by Social Alpha.
What this proves: We can take a complex, technical, and politically charged subject — the textile industry's relationship with colour, material, and people — and make it emotionally legible without simplifying it. That is exactly what the Saamuhika Shakti report and video deliverable require.
02
Illustration
Kempegowda — History and culture channel, 400K+ subscribers
Editorial narrative illustration
A large-format narrative illustration commissioned for a Bengaluru history and culture channel. The brief: compress the story of Bengaluru's founder, its heritage architecture, its colonial legacy, and its modern infrastructure into one cohesive image — one that could scale from a book cover to a t-shirt to digital content without losing its integrity.

The illustration layers historical and contemporary references in a single composition. The Kempegowda portrait anchors the frame. Below him, a horse-drawn carriage shares space with a Namma Metro train. Colonial-era buildings sit alongside the Vidhana Soudha. Human-scale figures ground the city in lived experience. Selective colour — ink linework with green and orange fills — signals a considered aesthetic choice, not a default.
What this proves: B-Moat can hold a complex, multi-layered narrative in a single visual frame with editorial confidence and human detail. The textile waste lifecycle illustration — the journey of a garment from household to waste picker to collection centre to recycler to new product — requires exactly this capability.
03
NGO Web
Yusuf Meherally Centre
Full-cycle NGO web engagement
Yusuf Meherally Centre is a voluntary rural development organisation founded in memory of freedom fighter Yusuf Meherally. It runs NABARD programmes, community training, women's self-help groups, and livelihood initiatives across Maharashtra. It is supported by Tara Trust.

When they came to B-Moat, their WordPress site was unsecure, fragmented across multiple hosting owners, and effectively non-functional. There was no single person who controlled the domain.

B-Moat tracked down the hosting trail, recovered access through multiple owners, rebuilt the site on Framer, worked with the YMC team and partner organisations through multiple rounds of review, delivered a functional site, and concluded the engagement with a formal knowledge transfer session — ensuring the YMC team could run the site independently, without ongoing dependence on us.
What this proves: We know how to work with mission-driven organisations — multiple stakeholders, fragmented infrastructure, tight resources. We stay patient. We stay structured. We deliver. The Saamuhika Shakti project involves coordinating across twelve partner organisations — this is a familiar environment for us.
04
Web
Bookaloom — Handwoven textile artisan platform
End-to-end web design and development
Bookaloom is a platform connecting handwoven textile artisans across India — from Kashmir to Kancheepuram — to global buyers and designers. The brief centred on one specific goal: driving footfall to physical experience centres where buyers could meet the weavers, see the fabrics, and understand the craft behind them.

B-Moat handled the project end to end. UX strategy, Figma design, Shopify build, and custom development. When the client's copy fell short at the last moment, we worked with what was available and produced the website copy under a tight deadline to get the project across the line.
What this proves: The subject matter is the closest in our portfolio to Saamuhika Shakti's world — handwoven textiles, artisan livelihoods, sustainable sourcing, and the value of skilled craft in a supply chain. The design prioritised warmth and the human behind the product over commercial transaction. That sensibility is what the Saamuhika Shakti web page needs.
05
Copy & Voice
Earthworks — Circular materials company
Brand voice and tone of voice framework
Earthworks transforms agricultural waste — jute fibre, areca husks, bamboo residue — into bio-composite granules for industrial manufacturing. They replace conventional plastics without requiring factories to change their equipment. The brand sits at the intersection of material science and sustainability — a space crowded with inflated claims and empty language.

B-Moat developed the complete brand voice framework: personality definition, key messaging hierarchy, tone of voice pillars, a voice matrix with explicit do and don't examples, copy examples across contexts, an anti-tone section, and a usage guide.

The strategic challenge was building a sustainability brand voice that earns trust rather than performing it. The anti-tone section explicitly rejects words like "revolutionary," "planet-saving," and "game-changing" in favour of language grounded in verifiable data, material science, and the quiet confidence of people who have actually done the work.
What this proves: The Saamuhika Shakti report needs to hold a precise tonal register across three audiences simultaneously — the impact sector, textile manufacturers, and policy officials. It must be warm without being sentimental, rigorous without being academic, and hopeful without minimising what is genuinely difficult. The Earthworks document shows we understand how to build, define, and protect exactly that kind of voice.
06
Decks
IoTreat / Ecoterra / ISM — Early stage pitch decks
Structured narrative design for sustainability ventures
ISM / Ecoterra slides
Three pitch decks for early stage ventures in adjacent spaces — IoTreat in healthcare infrastructure, Ecoterra in sustainable materials, and ISM in multilayer packaging recycling and circular economy. Each deck was built for founders who had deep domain expertise but needed help translating it into a clear, compelling narrative for external audiences.

B-Moat worked with each founding team on concept, narrative structure, copy, and design. The decks were intentionally left partially open — the founders wanted space to make final edits themselves — so what you see represents the finished narrative scaffolding and design system, not a complete final deck.

The problem statements are where the writing earns its keep. "Infrastructure exists. But its utilisation is broken." "The materials used to improve our comfort harm our health." These are not taglines — they are arguments compressed into a single sentence.
Problem / solution slides
What this proves: Long-form visual documents with a clear narrative arc, designed for audiences that need to be persuaded rather than simply informed. The same structural thinking — problem, evidence, system, proof, invitation — is the backbone of the Saamuhika Shakti report.

06 — How we'd approach this

A first sketch of the story spine.

This is not a treatment or a proposal. It's an early-stage narrative sketch — what happens when we spend a few hours with your articles, case studies, and brief, and ask: what is the irreducible story this report needs to tell?

We offer it to show you how we think, not to pre-empt the collaborative process that would refine it significantly.

  • The shirt you gave away
    Open with the object everyone knows — a garment they've discarded. Where did it go? Most people have no idea. This section follows the shirt out of the front door and into the city's invisible infrastructure.
  • The hands that hold the system together
    Introduce the waste picker — not as a problem to be solved but as the expert the system depends on. First-person voice. Specific, named, Bengaluru-rooted. This is where the report earns its right to make the circularity argument.
  • Where it breaks down
    Honest about the system's failures — the 93% of used textiles that don't re-enter the circular economy, the market failures, the infrastructure gaps. Data-led, illustrated as a flow. This section is what makes the report credible to impact investors and policymakers.
  • What the collective built
    The Saamuhika Shakti intervention — CAIF, Hasiru Dala, Social Alpha, the button story, the DWCC operators, the collection drives. Told as a system map, not a list of activities. How each partner owned a node in the chain. What changed when they worked together.
  • What circularity looks like when it includes everyone
    The proof. Quantified impact, community voices, the traceable button on an H&M garment. This section is the argument made visible — people and planet, not in tension but in partnership. The model that can be replicated.
  • The invitation
    Close with a call to action calibrated to each audience: the brand, the investor, the policymaker, the citizen. Concrete, specific, and grounded in what the reader can actually do with what they've just learned.
